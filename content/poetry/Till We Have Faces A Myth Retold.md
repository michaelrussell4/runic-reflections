Title: Till We Have Faces: A Myth Retold

Category: Reviews
Author: Mike Russell
Tags: reflective, philosophical, existential, religious

## 5/5 Stars ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

I just finished *Till We Have Faces: A Myth Retold*, by C. S. Lewis. I can hardly give any higher praise for a book. It is a masterpiece. However, I'm not sure this book is for most people. Perhaps for the very reasons I loved it others would not. Regardless, while the content is fresh on my mind, I'd like to jot down, if only for my own memory's sake, the things I love about it. I can't promise to avoid spoilers as this would require more attention that I'd like to put into this and might deprive me of fully expressing my appreciation.

## Uncompromised realism

C. S. Lewis' ability to capture the essence of his archaic setting and stay true to it throughout his book was a pleasant surprise. As I read I wondered, "Is this the same guy who wrote those cheesy, conspicuous metaphors: The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe?—much too on-the-nose! I don't want propaganda, I want the truth, no matter how uncomfortable or unconventional. Lewis made me feel that the characters could really have been plucked from the small kingdom of Glome some few hundred years before common era.

Where a modern writer would poison a story with the modern stench of feminism, egalitarianism, political expressiveness, etc., Lewis delivered only an objective telling so convincingly that it can hardly be believed he was not a native of this fictitious land. And this is coming from a man with a keen nose for that modern stink of which I speak—it has, for better or worse, completely ruined most all modern art for me, cinematic or written, so sensitive my nose, much how once you smell the rotten odor, your appetite is gone and you can't un-smell it.

## Ingenious fitting to the Psyche and Cupid Myth

I was unfamiliar with the Greek myth of Psyche and Cupid. I just read up on the summary and I'm baffled at how well Lewis was able to build out this myth, as if its origin had truly come from him and not the ancients. This book could well have been its true origin and our world would be quite unchanged, so masterful a job Lewis did.

## Parallels and contrasts

Orual and Istra are mother and child, ugliness and beauty, maturity and naïveté, intellectualism and faith, cynical and gullible, selfishness and selflessness, sufferer and enjoyer. The parallels are abundant as the archetypes are so well crafted. The characters of the book make for so many types and applications. It's beautiful. Who won't claim that Orual's words are their own when she wrestles against god and man—capturing the heart of the struggles of our moral failings when it comes to grasping that which we don't understand.

## Approaching the taboo

Lewis does not fear to approach topics and themes that many would repulse at, much less entertain with intimate familiarity. Perhaps this is what gives the book its mature edge. I laugh, or wince, at the thought of his fans coming to expect something like the Chronicles of Narnia or Mere Christianity with their blatant, preachy, sanitized talk and teaching, only to be met with the unrelenting struggle against imperfect knowledge, unfairness, unanswered questions, death, suffering, violence, abuse, and instead of placating with modern platitudes, he answers with the objective crudeness nature alone had been willing to bear. Yet, in a way few could do, he paints it so as to offer some satisfactory meaning and sense through it all, leaving one in pensive quietude rather than gnawing nihilism.
