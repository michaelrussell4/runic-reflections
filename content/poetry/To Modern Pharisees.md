When I get explanations of the Trinity from members of mainstream Christian denominations, the response usually resembles Modalism more than Trinitarianism. Most members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints likewise carry misunderstanding of the Trinity. At the same time, figures claiming to represent Christian orthodoxy accuse Latter-day Saints of having scripture that teaches Modalism. The result is ironic:

- Many mainstream Christians describe their belief in the Trinity as Modalism
- Other Christians accuse Latter-day Saints of not being Christian because their scripture teaches Modalism
- Latter-day Saints unanimously reject Modalism and believe in the Godhead, which is closer to Trinitarianism than Modalism

If you don't know what Modalism is, you're not alone. Some definitions are in order:

- **Modalism (heresy)**: God is a single person who manifests himself in three different roles throughout time: Father, Son, and Holy Ghost.
- **Trinity (orthodox)**: God is three distinct *persons* who share the same essence and are hence one *being*.
- **Godhead (LDS)**: the Godhead consists of three distinct *persons* who are unified in purpose, mind, and nature, but are not the same being.

One might ask, "Does it really matter? God is a mystery so let's just focus on Jesus and set this aside." Straining at the complexities of an incomprehensible God could lead to unnecessary divisions and distractions. Essentially, Christianity is that we, the fallen race of Adam, may be saved through faith in Christ, the Son of God, who became mortal, died, and rose again from the dead. In this there is little disagreement. Beyond this, there's no shortage of disagreement. Yet, while the core message of Christianity ought to unite us, differences in specifics result in substantial divergences. Consider Calvinism and Arminianism, the theological differences between which make them seem categorically opposed. The subtleties, therefore, may accumulate to substantial importance, making discussions on what seem petty matters, matter.

As mentioned, mainstream Christian gatekeepers have attempted to push the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints out of the Christian umbrella. As a Latter-day Saint myself, I feel that this is an affront to my Christ-centered faith and devotions. Since I've exerted considerable dedication to my faith in Christ, being told that I can't label myself with the universal Christ-follower label, "Christian" is bothersome. Sure, my faith isn't dictated by labels, but the indication behind the labels conveys a lot, to the subject and society using them. However, I concede that when juxtaposed with mainstream Christian sects, we Latter-day Saints may seem like the platypus of the mammalian world.

The primary difference driving this labeling matter is our beliefs on the nature of God and the Trinity. Our conception of God is that God is three distinct personages—the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost—who are completely united in purpose and mind. We refer to them as the Godhead, a divine presidency. But just as with the Trinitarian conception of God, distinguishing between the persons in this union is not always trivial. We interpret most "God" references in scripture as referring to Jesus Christ, the Son, whom we also to believe to be Jehovah, or Yahweh. Instances of God the Father in scripture are more rare, almost exclusively to introduce or speak of His Son. The Holy Ghost is referenced as the Spirit, the influence and mind of God. The Son is often called the Father, due to unique symbolism or context, adding to the complexity.

For the average Christian, the initial issue with this interpretation is its divergence from the long-standing Trinity doctrine. Additionally, many point out how three Gods is polytheistic.

## Non-Trinity Conceptions

To address the divergence from tradition, I must point out that the Trinity is not explicitly taught in the Bible and prior to the councils of Nicaea (325 AD) and Constantinople (381 AD), various theological frameworks existed among early Christians. Imagine how odd it would be for a modern Christian to go back in time and tell one of these O.G. Christians, who could likely suffer martyrdom for their belief, that they weren't Christian because they didn't believe in the Trinity!

- The Father existing before and without the Son: "There was, however, a time when neither sin existed with Him, nor the Son" [(Tertullian, ANF03 Chapter III)](https://ccel.org/ccel/tertullian/against_hermogenes/anf03.v.v.iii.html)
- The Son being subordinate in rank and divine by participation, rather than being the ultimate source of divinity: Origen of Alexandria argued that only the Father is *Autotheos* ("Very God" or "God of Himself"), while the Son is *Theos* without the definite article because His divinity is derived by remaining in "uninterrupted contemplation of the depths of the Father." Addressing those who feared this distinction would mean "proclaiming two Gods," Origen explained that "all beyond the Very God is made God by participation in His divinity." [(Origen, Commentary on John, Book II, Chapter 2)](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/101502.htm)

There are plenty more where these came from, but suffice it to say that many non-traditional views on God have existed, before and after, the adoption of the Trinity into orthodoxy.

I have to again draw attention to the misunderstanding of the Trinity within mainstream Christianity. If strict adherence to the creedal Trinity is requisite for Christian membership, Latter-day Saints would be the least of those excluded. Surveys show that most Christians don't understand or believe in strict Trinitarianism [(The State of Theology)](https://thestateoftheology.com/). Ironically, one of the most common analogies Christians use to explain the Trinity is the water analogy—"God is like water, He can be ice, liquid, or steam, but He's still H₂O." The average Christian actively teaches Modalism without realizing it [(Modalism | Ligonier.org)](https://learn.ligonier.org/podcasts/simply-put/modalism). The irony is that LDS members' Godhead conception, also known as Social Trinitarianism, is arguably more aligned with Trinitarianism than Modalism, making the Trinity gatekeeping highly inconsistent.

## Polytheism

As for the polytheism problem, we actually believe there are a lot more than three Gods. We believe that "we are the offspring of God" (Acts 17:28-29), created "...in the image of God" (Genesis 1:27). Consistent to these principles, we believe our potential is to become "heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ" (Romans 8:17), "to sit with [Christ] in [his] throne, even as [he] also overcame, and set down with [his] Father in his throne" (Revelation 3:21). God's work includes raising seed to become like Him. This alone implies a possibility for countless divine beings. This participation in divinity is not a new idea; it is the ancient Christian doctrine of theosis (deification). Origen of Alexandria touched on this in the source referenced above, and it remains a foundational pillar within Eastern Orthodox theology.

We have no doctrinal mandates to assume that there could be no other divine beings beside God. A common response is, "...there is no God; I know not any" (Isaiah 44:8), a scripture misunderstood to be stating that no gods exist apart from God. However, this is an instance of the non-comparable boastful claim common during the Babylonian exile era. We also see it used in Isaiah 47:8, "...thou that art given to pleasures, that dwellest carelessly, that sayest in thine heart, I am, and none else beside me". Obviously, we would not think that this identified individual is claiming that they're the only being in existence, they're merely shown demonstrating use of that same boastful idiom. Hence, usage of these scriptures to suggest that the Bible teaches no gods existing beside God is an error, based on misinterpretation.

It surely will be a surprise to some, but there are no scriptures claiming that no divine beings exist beside God. On the contrary, there are several references pointing to a multiplicity of gods, or divine beings:

- The Divine Council (Psalm 82:1): "God [Elohim] standeth in the congregation of the mighty; he judgeth among the gods [elohim]."
- The Sons of God (Job 1:6, 38:7): The text describes the Bene Elohim ("sons of God" or "divine sons") shouting for joy at the creation of the world.
- Comparison Passages (Psalm 95:3, Exodus 15:11): "For the Lord is a great God, and a great King above all gods." If no other divine beings exist, comparing Yahweh to them becomes meaningless.

## A word on the Trinity

The Trinity is so complex that any attempt to articulate it sufficiently has fallen short. Most theologians have been left concluding it's just an unfathomable mystery that humans could never understand. The particular point of confusion is this: if the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are each distinct persons, how can they be one ontological being? The premises fail basic logic. If the Father is God, and the Son is God, then it cannot be true that the Father is not the Son. Yet, this is the claim of the creeds. The brightest minds have exerted themselves trying to explain precisely how to answer this question and have failed to sufficiently reconcile it.

Further issues arise when attempting to harmonize this doctrine with the presence of difference in will, "not my will, but thine, be done" (Luke 22:42). Jesus's intercessory prayer includes his plea to the Father for his disciples, "that they may be one, as we are" (John 17:11) To be consistent, Trinitarians would have to extend their ontological interpretation of oneness to this verse, resulting in the idea that Christ's disciples would become additional persons of the Being of God.

As if this weren't enough, integrating the incarnation of one person of this Being while the other two persons are unaffected only exacerbates this enigma. It's no wonder Aquinas stated: "It is impossible to attain to the knowledge of the Trinity by natural reason" [(Summa Theologiae, Ia, q.32, a.1)](https://www.newadvent.org/summa/1032.htm).

How apt Joseph Smith's words become, "If we start right, it is easy to go right all the time; but if we start wrong, it is a hard matter to get right" (King Follett Discourse). When an assumption leads to so much incongruence, it may be time to reconsider that assumption. The LDS conception of God, I must add, results in none of this inconsistency.

## Conclusion

We arrive here with the following conclusions:

- Historical Inconsistency: Non-Trinitarian frameworks were held by prominent pre-Nicene Christians, making it historically inaccurate to require post-Nicene creedal adherence as a prerequisite for the generic label "Christian."
- Functional Contradiction: Mainstream Christians frequently default to Modalistic analogies, meaning they routinely practice and teach the very concept they label a heresy.
- Structural Alignment: The LDS Godhead framework (Social Trinitarianism) preserves the distinct personhood of the Father, Son, and Spirit, placing it closer to actual Trinitarian intent than popular Modalism.
- Biblical Precedent: The biblical text explicitly supports, rather than refutes, the existence of a divine council and a multiplicity of divine beings subordinate to the Father.

This basic description of the LDS conception of God and defense against common objections will hopefully provide a basis for reconsideration of what the term "Christian" means and whether it's appropriate to exclude the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints from the category.

In an age where division is prevalent and secularism conspires against traditional values and Christianity, unification of the good-hearted, the charitable, the faithful, and the pure should be encouraged. Pedantic and petty pursuits are a waste of time. For those willing to examine without prejudice or pride, I think you'll find the members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints to bear the fruit of the Spirit spoken of by Paul: "love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith" (Galatians 5:22). For those who think the presence of such in our lives and scripture is a pretense setup by demonic or ungodly forces, I respond with Jesus's own words to a similar accusation:
