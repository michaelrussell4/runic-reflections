Title: Till We Have Faces: A Myth Retold
Date: 2026/06/29 06:57PM
Category: Reviews
Author: Mike Russell
Tags: reflective, philosophical, existential, religious

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

I just finished *Till We Have Faces: A Myth Retold*, by C. S. Lewis. I can hardly give any higher praise for a book. It is a masterpiece. However, I'm not sure this book is for everyone. Perhaps for the very reasons I loved it, others would not. Allegedly, the book was a commercial flop as Lewis's fans gave a muted and confused reception while his critics, who might've appreciated it, had already dismissed him as a Christian apologist whose novels weren't worth their time. Yet, Lewis said it was "far and away [his] best book". How I do agree, though my credentials be lacking as I haven't read half his books.

Regardless, while the content is fresh on my mind, I'd like to jot down, if only for my own sake, the things I love about it. I can't promise to avoid spoilers as this would require tedious attention and might deprive me of fully expressing my appreciation.

### Uncompromised realism

C. S. Lewis's ability to capture the essence of his archaic setting and stay true to it throughout his book was a pleasant surprise. As I read I wondered, "Is this the same guy who wrote those cheesy, conspicuous metaphors such as in The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe?—much too on-the-nose! I prefer cryptic symbols loosely applied on a backdrop as messy and brutal as reality, no matter how uncomfortable or unconventional—Lewis here offered exactly that. He made me feel that the characters could really have been plucked from the small kingdom of Glome some few hundred years before common era.

Where a modern writer would knowingly or unknowingly poison a story with the stench of contemporary ideologies: feminism, egalitarianism, political expressiveness, etc., Lewis's delivery is so pure and untainted by such biases that it can hardly be believed he was not a native of this fictional land telling a story he'd witnessed first-hand.

Granted, Lewis cannot be mistaken as a man of modernity; that WWI veteran is more a product of classical 19th-century brilliance, a relic of the golden age. Perhaps it's because of his exposure to the savageness of war, extremity of suffering, and severity of loss that he so well writes this tale. He speaks with an understanding very few have today, a disparity that worsens when filtering down to writers.

### Ingenious fitting to the Psyche and Cupid Myth

I was unfamiliar with the Greek myth of Psyche and Cupid. I just read up on the summary and I'm baffled at how well Lewis was able to build out this myth, as if its origin had truly come from him and not the ancients. This book could well have been its true origin and none would know better, so masterful a job Lewis did.

### Parallels and contrasts

Orual and Istra are mother and child, ugliness and beauty, loved and hated, maturity and naïveté, intellectual and faithful, cynical and gullible, selfish and selfless, sufferer and enjoyer. The parallels are abundant as the archetypes are so well crafted. The characters of the book make for myriad types and applications. It's beautiful. Who won't claim that Orual's words are their own when she wrestles against god and flesh—capturing the struggle man reaching for god.

Lysias is the cunning Fox; the priest of Ungit is a bird. The implications are apt: the fox represents earthly wisdom and understanding but is earth-bound, unable to comprehend the ethereal mysteries. Conversely, the priest represents exactly that mystical, unreachable domain. Just as the birds fly and go where mortals can't (couldn't), so the priest glimpses what's beyond. The bird and the fox do not get along well—a beautiful pair in this tale.

### Approaching the taboo

Lewis does not fear to approach topics and themes that many would repulse at, much less entertain with intimate familiarity. Perhaps this is what gives the book its mature edge. I laugh, or wince, at the thought of his fans coming to expect something like the Chronicles of Narnia or Mere Christianity with their blatant, preachy, sanitized talk and teaching, only to be met with the unrelenting struggle against imperfect knowledge, unfairness, unanswered questions, death, suffering, violence, abuse. And instead of placating with modern platitudes, he answers with objective, but poignantly accurate, crudeness. Yet, in a way few could do, he paints it so as to offer a fulfilling sense of meaning through it all, leaving one in pensive quietude rather than gnawing nihilism. And that is the essence of art.
