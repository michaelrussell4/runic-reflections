Title: Presiding in Power: A Correction to the Modern Conception of the Man
Date: 2026/02/19 07:38PM
Category: Essays
Tags: religious, serious

What does it mean for a man to preside in his home? This traditional role was pragmatic and functional in the past; however, modernity has introduced new dynamics that leave some questioning whether the preside-protect-provide role of fathers still holds value or relevance. For Christians, particularly members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, this role has the support of scripture and ecclesiastical authority, making it difficult to ignore.

Individuals' responses to this mandate generally fall into one of three categories:

1. **Orthodoxy:** Accepting the role as traditionally and scripturally understood.  
2. **Rejection:** Dismissing the role, often resulting in fragmented or abandoned religious beliefs.  
3. **Reinterpretation:** Modifying the role to be compatible with modern egalitarian views.

In my experience, a majority of individuals, including prominent church figures, have engaged to some degree in this third category. I will describe the manifestations of this trend and examine whether such reinterpretation can be reconciled doctrinally and logically.

### The Presence of Reinterpretation

One form of reinterpretation appears as a softening of the term "preside" while magnifying "equal partnership." To illustrate the linguistic tension, consider the following definitions:

* **Webster’s Dictionary 1828:** To be set over for the exercise of authority; to direct, control and govern, as the chief officer.  
* **Merriam-Webster:** To exercise guidance, direction, or control; to occupy the place of authority: act as president, chairperson, or moderator.

The meaning of this word has not changed significantly in 200 years. Authority is its key component. Furthermore, a president presides; the two share an etymological root. To suggest that one "presides" but is not the functional equivalent of a "president" is a lexical contradiction. This contradiction arises when rhetoric attempts to satisfy modern sentiments at the expense of definitions. For example, Elder Soares stated: "...spouses... do not position themselves as president or vice president of their family. There is no superiority or inferiority in the marriage relationship" (*In Partnership with the Lord*, 2022). This suggests the role of "president" is an inappropriate term, stripping the hierarchical status away from the act of presiding.

### Function vs. Value

In societies past, a difference in function did not equate to a difference in value. However, influenced by feminism and social activism, modern society has accepted a version of egalitarianism that conflates the two. This view makes any functional difference between a husband and wife appear as a threat to fairness. Consequently, modern rhetoric often presents a version of presiding that is stripped of its fundamental components—higher power, authority, or status—rendering the role nominal and vestigial.

We see this even in well-meaning teachings. President Hunter (1994) noted that "meet" means equal, and that presiding necessitates "shared responsibility." While intended to reconcile the tension in the term "helpmeet," this often results in a divergence from the traditional understanding. Abandoning the traditional approach in favor of a milder interpretation merely mitigates contemporary tension without addressing the underlying logical requirements of leadership.

"Help meet" is not a derogatory title; it describes one who provides vital aid (‘ezer), a term scripturally applied to God. Recent modifications to the LDS Temple presentation of the Adam and Eve motif now apply the term reciprocally, stating they are to be "help meets for each other." This shift suggests an attempt to neutralize a perceived derogatory connotation by making the role symmetrical. Such revisions prioritize the mitigation of social tension over the preservation of the original, functional distinctions established in the text.

### The Logic of Accountability

Modern reinterpretation creates an ironic result: the man is held more accountable than his family, yet is granted no higher authority than them. Logically, this is an unsustainable position. It is unjust to hold an individual more accountable for an outcome unless they possess the executive power to influence it.

The scriptural and doctrinal foundations remain clear on this hierarchical structure:

* **Ephesians 5:22-25:** "For the husband is the head of the wife even as Christ is the head of the church... as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit in everything to their husbands."  
* **The Family Proclamation:** "By divine design, fathers are to preside over their families... mothers are primarily responsible for the nurture of their children."

In accordance with logic, to preside means to hold a higher authority and higher executive power. This justifies the higher accountability. A man who presides takes primary responsibility for the direction of his family; he functions as the designated tie-breaker for important decisions.

### Conclusion

Authority and power can be abused, and the role of presiding is not immune to corruption. Guidance from scriptures and leaders directs fathers to preside in gentleness and righteousness; if a man exercises "unrighteous dominion," he loses his authority. However, the potential for abuse does not negate the necessity of the office.

Men preside by God’s design. Wives and children are to submit to that direction as the Church does to Christ. A wise man consults his wife, but the final decision—and the weight of its responsibility—is his. As Christ demonstrated, the master is he who serves righteously. When we water down this role to fit modern political biases, we do a disservice to men, to the scriptures, and to the logic of the family unit itself.

This address is not to the world, but to Christians, especially Latter-day Saints who have by intention or mistake accepted an erroneous view of what it means to preside.
